Paxlovid & Dysgeusia: Investigating Taste Disturbances from COVID-19 Treatment

Designed and conducted sensory testing to isolate which component of Paxlovid (Nirmatrelvir vs. Ritonavir) causes taste distortion, and linked findings to known bitter taste receptor biology.

R Clinical Trial Design Sensory Testing Statistical Analysis R Markdown

Background

What Is Dysgeusia and Why Does It Matter?

Paxlovid (Nirmatrelvir + Ritonavir) is a widely prescribed oral antiviral treatment for COVID-19. A commonly reported side effect is dysgeusia — a persistent, often metallic or bitter distortion of taste that can make eating unpleasant and reduce patient adherence to the full treatment course.

Despite how frequently this side effect is reported, the specific mechanism and which component of the drug causes it were not well characterized. This internship project at the Monell Chemical Senses Center — the world's leading independent institute for taste and smell research — aimed to fill that gap.

Methods

How the Study Worked

1

Solution Preparation

Prepared taste solutions of Nirmatrelvir alone, Ritonavir alone, and a combination — dissolved in 5% ethanol to ensure consistent bioavailability and taste delivery across all conditions.

2

Controlled Taste-Intensity Testing

Participants rated the perceived taste intensity of each solution on a standardized scale. Tests were conducted under blinded conditions to prevent expectation bias.

3

Order Counterbalancing

Presentation order of the three solutions was varied across participants to eliminate carry-over and ordering effects. Statistical tests confirmed no significant order effect was present.

4

Group-Level & Individual-Level Analysis

Analyzed both mean intensity ratings across conditions and individual-level variation to characterize the distribution of taste sensitivity and identify outlier responders.

5

Receptor Biology Review

Linked experimental findings to the known pharmacology of bitter taste receptors, particularly TAS2R7, to explain the molecular basis for observed taste disturbances.

Results

What We Found

Nirmatrelvir is the primary driver

Nirmatrelvir alone and the combination solution produced significantly higher average taste intensity than Ritonavir alone — identifying the main culprit in Paxlovid dysgeusia.

No ordering bias detected

Statistical testing showed no significant correlation between the order solutions were presented and the intensity ratings — confirming the counterbalancing design worked.

TAS2R7 receptor implicated

The pattern of intensity responses is consistent with activation of TAS2R7, a bitter taste receptor known to respond to protease inhibitor compounds.

Reproducible across participants

Individual-level data showed consistent direction of effect — the higher intensity of Nirmatrelvir solutions held across participants despite variation in absolute sensitivity.

Takeaway

By isolating Nirmatrelvir as the primary driver of Paxlovid dysgeusia and linking the finding to TAS2R7 bitter taste receptor activation, this study provides a mechanistic explanation for a commonly reported but poorly understood side effect — with implications for future formulation design and patient counseling around COVID-19 treatment adherence.
