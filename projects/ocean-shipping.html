Does International Shipping Affect Ocean Nitrate & Phytoplankton?

A multi-method analysis of shipping lane proximity, nitrate concentrations, and chlorophyll-a levels across the North Atlantic.

Python K-Means Clustering OLS Regression Random Forest Spatial Block CV ERDDAP / NOAA API

The Question

Research Motivation

International shipping is a major source of nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions. When these compounds dissolve into seawater they can act as fertilizers, potentially elevating nitrate concentrations and stimulating phytoplankton growth — or, in already nutrient-rich areas, exacerbating hypoxia. Despite the scale of global shipping, this chemical footprint on ocean biology is poorly quantified.

This project asks: Are areas with higher shipping density measurably different in their nitrate and chlorophyll-a concentrations, after controlling for geography, season, and sea surface temperature?

Methods

Analytical Pipeline

1

Data Cleaning & Feature Engineering

Removed physically implausible values (nitrate >60 mmol/m³), aggregated 6.8M depth-level rows to 29,913 unique stations, and added log-transformed features and cyclical month encoding (sin/cos) for seasonal patterns.

2

Longhurst Province Assignment

Each station was assigned to an oceanographic biogeochemical province (e.g., North Atlantic Drift, Canary Current Upwelling, NE Atlantic Shelf) to capture ecological context without leaking raw geography into the models.

3

SST Integration via NOAA ERDDAP API

Downloaded 276 months of OISST data (2002–2024) and joined to observations at 1-degree spatial resolution — a key fix that raised the match rate from 12% to 52%.

4

K-Means Clustering with Triple Validation

Chose the optimal k via majority vote across elbow (inertia), silhouette score, and Davies-Bouldin index. Data supported k=2: coastal shelf vs. open ocean — an ecologically meaningful divide.

5

Province-Stratified OLS Regression

Ran separate regressions per province with HC3 robust standard errors, controlling for distance-to-coast, season, and SST. Computed partial R² for shipping to isolate its unique contribution.

6

Random Forest with Spatial Block Cross-Validation

Used 5°×5° geographic blocks as CV folds — holding out entire regions rather than random rows — to prevent spatial autocorrelation from inflating R². Reported permutation importance on held-out blocks only.

Results

Key Findings

Shipping signal is real but small

Shipping density has a statistically significant association with nitrate in most provinces (p < 0.001), but its partial R² is only 3–6% — far below geography and season.

Direction varies by province

NE Atlantic Shelf: positive association (beta = +0.023). Subarctic Atlantic: negative association (beta = -0.023). No one-size-fits-all relationship.

Nitrate model generalizes (R² = 0.32)

Spatial block CV R² of 0.32 for nitrate confirms the model learned something real — not memorized geography. An honest benchmark for this kind of spatial holdout.

Geography dominates

Random forest permutation importance shows distance-to-coast, province, and season consistently outrank shipping density. Shipping is a secondary — not primary — predictor.

Chlorophyll-a harder to predict

Strong regional character means the chlor-a model fails to generalize across geographic blocks (some folds: R² < -90), a sign that spatial autocorrelation dominates the signal.

Non-linear effects present

Partial dependence plots show a threshold effect in the Iberian coastal zone — the shipping-nitrate relationship only strengthens at the highest traffic densities.

Takeaway

International shipping has a detectable but modest association with ocean nitrate levels — present in most North Atlantic provinces, statistically significant, but explaining only a few percent of variance beyond geography and season. The ocean's chemistry is driven primarily by circulation, upwelling, and latitude. Shipping is a real second-order signal, not the dominant force — and its direction depends heavily on local ecological context.

Limitations & Future Work

What We'd Do Next

The shipping density data is a static raster aggregated across years, while nitrate measurements are point-in-time — this temporal mismatch likely suppresses detectable effects. Dynamic AIS vessel tracking data matched to nitrate observations by date would be a major improvement.

Ocean currents and atmospheric deposition of nitrogen are major nitrate pathways not captured here. Incorporating reanalysis products (e.g., HYCOM current fields, MERRA-2 atmospheric NOx deposition) would help separate the shipping footprint from background oceanographic forcing.

Future work could leverage satellite-derived NO2 columns (Sentinel-5P TROPOMI) directly above shipping lanes as a more direct measure of atmospheric nitrogen loading to the surface ocean.
